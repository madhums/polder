Water Complexity Lab

December 12, 2023 by admin

Duration: Jan 2024 – Dec 2028

Location: Amsterdam metropolitan area

Our project focuses on the interactions among the various water users and uses in the Metropolitan Region of Amsterdam (MRA). The MRA is home to around 2.5 million people living in 32 municipalities and two provinces. As the largest municipality and economic hub with one of the country’s major ports, Amsterdam is a dominant factor regarding how water is used and managed in the MRA. The city is part of the EU’s 100 Cities Mission to achieve being Net Zero, Smart and Circular by 2030. It furthermore aims to become a “Doughnut Economy”, i.e., an economy in which societal needs are met, including those of marginalized communities, while staying within the “Planetary Boundaries”, and to work towards the internationally agreed-upon United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). However, it is currently unclear how these goals can be aligned with and contribute to plans and strategies regarding water services safety and quality at regional and national scale. And there is a lack of knowledge on how to adequately intervene to transform the currently unsustainable system and achieve these aspirations without creating tradeoffs across different water-using sectors (e.g., water, food, energy).

Objective

Identify feasible pathways and robust intervention points towards resilient and sustainable urban water management systems by unravelling the interactions among their social, ecological and technological system elements and across sectors and scales.

Key 1

Map the system in its current state, including demand and supply across sectors, and its social (actors and institutions), ecological (water resource dynamics and quality, ecosystems), financial, and technological elements (built infrastructure); identify potential threats/risks to its resilience, and long-term sustainability challenges (future demand/supply, policies and risks).

Key 2

Create a co-creative space for policymakers, practitioners and scientists, where stakeholder needs and concerns are identified, and a shared vision towards a more sustainable water system is developed through participatory, multi-stakeholder dialogues.

Key 3

Co-develop an interactive model and simulation interface with stakeholders that can be used to identify suitable water policy/management interventions, bottlenecks and consequences impacting water services, urban planning, and other sectors.
